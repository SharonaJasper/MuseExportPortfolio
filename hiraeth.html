Hiraeth Bench

"Many Welsh people claim 'hiraeth' is a word which cannot be translated, meaning more than solely "missing something" or "missing home." To some, it implies the meaning of missing a time, an era, or a person. It is associated with the bittersweet memory of missing something or someone, while being grateful of that / their existence." -Wikipedia

 

 

This assignment involved designing a piece of street furniture for an existing public space. It had to have a purpose that fits the surroundings; solve a problem or be of value to that public space. While some people interpreted this as a practical problem, my approach was more towards a less visible problem.

 

The public space that I chose, the Westerbegraafplaats, is actually is a beautifully green cemetery, surrounded by busy streets and several companies. The beauty and serenity of the place, definitely does not match its surroundings and the way by-passers view it. The older visitors are often alone and younger visitors sometimes bring family with them. Most of this group of lonely older visitors have no interaction with anyone during their visit, which seems to be some kind of unwritten rule. This rule might be born out of respect or loss of words due to the ambiance, which does not match the friendly appearance of the place in the midst of a business park. This was the less visible problem I was talking about earlier.

Therefore the focus was on finding a gentle way to overrule this ambiance and the surroundings, but not damage the dignity of the place. The fact that this problem was not easily translatable and involved solving a particular feeling instead, inspired the name 'Hireath'. No matter what the object was going to be, this was the best discription for the feeling I wanted this object to create. 'Hiraeth' in the good sense of the word, bringing forward the beauty of memories in such a place.

The most gentle object to solve this problem with, seemed to be a public bench. For this bench to fit its surroundings and yet to draw some attention, the design was intimate, but the spot very present. The exact concept spot for this street furniture is directly in the open space after the entrance. This is the perfect spot since there is nothing placed there and from there visitors spread out across the cemetery, reaching most of them.

 

In this vision of modest design, the exterior of the Hireath Bench is kept simple. The shape is round and has deep grooves on the outside, as this seduces people to sit inside the circle, without signs of hard physical restrictions. These grooves are inspired by the serenity of a raindrop falling in the water. The coating material lightly shiny, making the shape a bit clearer and white to create a counterpart to the blackness of death. The spiral in which the shape of the bench keeps getting smaller, is a symbol for life: starting big and gradually getting smaller. Another feature made to resemble life, is the imbalance the bench implicates. This actual imbalance is to be realised with springs on the side where a user sits on the bench. To enable this feature, some of the legs of the bench should have a retractable and extendable mechanism.

 

The pattern beneath the grooves is a cell structure of small pipes, with a watery liquid inside of them, moving together with the imbalance of the bench. This liquid can essentially be light green, the colour of vividness and freshness, but should be changeable with the seasons or specific events. The main pipe of this pipe system is attached under the seat. The idea behind this is that the user can actually hear the watery liquid flowing towards them when sagging, like the heavy flow of thoughts reaching him. This imbalance is restored, when one or more people join the user sitting on the bench.
