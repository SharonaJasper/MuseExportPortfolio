Author Driven Design Project

'The use of metaphors in Author Driven Design'

 

The two design results from this assignment are:

 

A LifeCycle Interaction Waterboiler, for which the actual usage corresponds with  metaphors for the phases of a product life cycle.

 

A lightning chair for an open public space to enjoy looking at

approaching (thunder)storms.

Author Driven Design (ADD) is a design approach in which the author himself is the motivator. This approach can be seen as the counterpart of Demand Driven Design, where the author's motivations are led by a client instead. ADD therefore is described as putting the emotional over the functional and is often based on creative findings of the author, making the outcome unique and special.

 

Because the project is Author Driven, it has to come from a personal interest. With a personal analysis my own intrinsic motivation and interests became clear, one of them being Metaphors. On the other hand, the project is formulated in the scope of a scientific challenge. In other words a research had to be conducted for, through or with design. In this case research through design, which is why Findeli's framework (see figure) was applied. Therefore the research question was formulated as:

 

“To what extent can, looking from the designer’s perspective, the implementation of metaphors in Author Driven Design be useful in comparison to Demand Driven Design?”

 

and the design question as:

 

“How can one make a personal object from an unlikely combination of different fields, using a metaphor?”

The two combinations to be further elaborated were:

 

Lightning + Chair = Lightning Chair

Life Cycle + Water Boiler = LifeCycle Interaction Boiler (LIB)

 

In the Design Answer, different mappable features of these combinations were explored. For the lightning chair the essential feature became the capriciousness of lightning. For the LIB it was more difficult to distinguish one feature, since this involved the mapping of a process rather than an (intangible) object. However, the answer to this dilemma was dividing the process into states and transitions and putting this together with the user scenarios of a water boiler.

In short, these are the design decisions made:

 

For the Lightning Chair the decision was made to not stick to the shape of a chair, but somehow make it possible to sit on (not necessarily in a comfortable way).

The shape of the final chair was made to resemble that of a lightning striking twice.

The chosen material is a highly conductive metal, fitting the scenario of lightning striking. From this decision some questions could be raised, concerning the safety of usage during an actual thunderstorm. However, most park benches are in fact no safer, as they contain conductive materials and are often in open spaces as well. In fact, the appearance of the lightning chair does imply the danger more accurately than most park benches do. In addition, the floor panel is reflective, making the user aware of the (stormy) sky when looking down, either to enjoy it or be alerted.

 

For the LIB, the decision was made to distinguish three general states in the Life cycle of a water boiler; after usage, usage and before usage phase.

As a metaphor for the after usage phase, the exterior layer, with a damaged and old look, can roll up/out, only when the material is heated. The usage phase is represented as the layer beneath it. Contrary to the exterior layer, it is looking really new and shiny and will wear off a bit after some time. For the before usage state, this layer has a heat sensitive paint on it with the pattern of bubble wrap plastic, implying the newness of a recently purchased boiler.

 

As for the Research Answer, metaphor implementation can be useful in both design approaches. As can be seen in figure 7, the tangibility of source and target can be decisive in the type of metaphor mapping. DDD will have little use for making a point with explicit mapping, since it is interested in guiding users in finding the functionality or values of the design. ADD on the other hand, values emotion over functionality, wanting every user to interpret the design in their own way. Therefore this approach uses a broader spectrum of metaphor mappings and could have further use with this theory of tangibility.
